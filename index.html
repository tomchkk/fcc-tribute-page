The Humble Typewriter
– a tribute.

"An artificial machine or method for the impressing or transcribing of letters singly or progressively one after another."

Henry Mill

Known variously as the 'Pterotype', the 'Typographer', and even as the 'scribe harpsichord', the typewriter was invented no less than 52 times.

With inventions dating back to as early as 1575, it is clear that the need to be able to quickly and accurately produce formal paperwork and documents, without the expense and intricacy of printing, has long been a concern. With numerous iterations and innovations along the way, it was not until around 1910 that the mechanical typewriter arrived at some sort of standardised design.

By the 1980s the mechanical typewriter had been all but replaced by the word-processing typewriter. However, this innovation was short-lived and, with the advent of desktop publishing, word processors were largely replaced by personal computers by the 1990s.

Despite its descent into near obscelecence, the legacy of the typewriter is large. This machine gave us the QWERTY keyboard layout, the carriage return, as well as the shift and the tab keys, among many other concepts and terminology.

All is not lost for our humble typewriter though, as it may still be found fullfilling various typing duties around the world, particularly in places where access to electrical power is scarce. Typewriters have also seen a more recent resurgance in popularity amongst hipsters and other subcultures, a market currently being tapped by the world's first smart typewriter, the Freewrite.
